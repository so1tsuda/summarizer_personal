How to AI proof your job. If you've used the latest agentic AI tools like clawed code, you'll know we've crossed a threshold. Building functioning software or analyzing complex data has gone almost overnight from a scarce specialist skill to something routine and ubiquitous. Yesterday's blacksmiths, so the argument goes, are today's web developers and data scientists. But I'm [snorts] not sure that's necessarily true. For years, we've been told that STEM and coding are the most valuable skills for the 21st century economy. and demand for these roles has indeed been strong, but the evidence suggests it was not necessarily a straightforward story of the quantitative skills driving that success. A landmark 2017 study by Harvard economist David Demming found that social skills more than mathematical ones have seen the biggest rewards in the labor market. I've extended his analysis to the present day and the conclusion still holds. The jobs that have thrived aren't the purely mathematical ones. They're the roles combining quantitative ability with skills like collaboration, persuasion, and creative problem solving. Consultants, economists, doctors, even software developers themselves, whose jobs involve much more creativity and collaboration than is often appreciated. Meanwhile, highly mathematical jobs with less emphasis on working with others have seen much more muted growth. This is actually a reversal from a few decades ago. In 1980, people strong with numbers but weak on social skills outearned those who had strong soft skills but weaker quantitative abilities. Today that's flipped. It's interesting to consider all of this as AI starts to automate coding. In many ways, this finding could be reassuring. The best coders and data scientists have succeeded not simply because they can write functions and formulas, but because of the ideas, the teamwork, and the creative problem solving they brought to their coding. And if you stop to think, was it ever really the act of writing the code that was the fun part of the job, or was it the things that code enabled you to build and discover?
